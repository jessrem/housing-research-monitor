★ Paper of the Week · published May 25, 2026

Unlocking Mortgage Lock-In: Equilibrium Effects in a Spatial Housing Ladder Model

Julia Fonseca, Lu Liu & Pierre Mabille · NBER Working Paper 35237

Why it's interesting: Lock-in is usually told as a supply story, but this paper shows it raises net housing demand — "missing downsizers" stay in larger homes, especially in expensive areas, offsetting a third of the price decline higher rates would otherwise cause. A temporary rate hike lifts house prices 4.4% and rents 1.5% versus a no-lock-in world, while borrower mobility falls 25%. The sharpest takeaway: a $10k starter-home-seller tax credit costs an estimated $650,000 per marginal move, so demand-based responses to lock-in are poorly targeted.

This section is not a literature scan and deliberately excludes EIG's and Agglomerations' own writing — you write that. Below: the open design questions I think matter most, then non-EIG sources mapped to each.

Open design questions worth resolving

  1. Boundaries & eligibility: How are zones drawn and which parcels qualify? Tying eligibility to unbuilt zoned capacity vs. demand changes where zones actually produce.
  2. The by-right mechanism: What statutory language makes approval genuinely ministerial (non-discretionary), and how do you stop discretionary "poison pills" from creeping back in?
  3. Environmental review: How does by-right approval interact with NEPA/CEQA-style review — does the zone need a categorical exemption to deliver real certainty?
  4. Legal authority & preemption: Land use is a state/local police power; what is the constitutional footing for a federal role (spending-power incentives vs. mandates), and will state preemption survive court challenge?
  5. Infrastructure & financing: Who funds the roads, water, and schools new density needs — and can value capture / land value taxation pay for it without taxing the buildings you want?
  6. Measuring price, supply & displacement: What's the credible identification strategy to show a zone caused more supply (and what it did to rents and incumbents)?
  7. Political economy: Why do citywide reforms stall, and does an opt-in, results-rewarded design actually defuse local opposition — or just relocate it?

California SB 35 (and SB 423 making it permanent)

California Legislature · 2017 / 2023

Helps with Q2 (by-right mechanism): The leading real-world model of legally binding ministerial "checklist" approval — the actual statutory language that makes approval non-discretionary, and the template a federal RTBZ would adapt.

How SB 79 / SB 35 ministerial approval avoids CEQA review

CEQA Developments (Allen Matkins) · Sep 2025

Helps with Q3 (environmental review): Explains precisely how ministerial approval sidesteps CEQA — the mechanism an RTBZ would need to handle NEPA/CEQA exposure and deliver genuine certainty.

Montana's Housing Reform Survives Constitutional Challenge

State Court Report · March 2026

Helps with Q4 (legal authority & preemption): A live test of whether state preemption of local zoning holds up in court — directly informs the federalism and constitutional-authority questions an RTBZ raises.

Assessing the Theory and Practice of Land Value Taxation

Lincoln Institute of Land Policy

Helps with Q5 (infrastructure financing): Foundational treatment of LVT and value capture — a candidate way to fund the infrastructure new density needs without taxing the structures you're trying to encourage.

The Effect of Rezoning on Local Housing Supply and Demand: Evidence from NYC

Regional Science and Urban Economics · 2026

Helps with Q6 (measuring effects): A worked identification strategy for isolating how rezoning changed supply, prices, and gentrification — a methodological template for evaluating an RTBZ.

Housing Reform in the States: A Menu of Options for 2026

Mercatus Center

Helps with Q7 (political economy) & Q1 (eligibility): Documents that legalization ≠ production (states legalized missing-middle housing but little got built) — the central risk an opt-in, results-rewarded zone is meant to overcome.

Data for your researchHousing Datasets

Census Building Permits Survey (BPS)

U.S. Census Bureau · ongoing (monthly & annual) · official

What's in it / why useful: Residential building permits by jurisdiction — the canonical series for tracking new-construction supply and permitting trends nationally and locally.

American Housing Survey (AHS)

U.S. Census Bureau / HUD · biennial · official

What's in it / why useful: Deep detail on the housing stock, costs, conditions, and tenure — the workhorse cross-section for affordability and housing-quality analysis.

National Zoning Atlas

Cornell-led (Sara Bronin) · ongoing · niche / still expanding — vet coverage

What's in it / why useful: 200+ regulatory characteristics per zoning district across ~3,000 jurisdictions — the best granular data on where by-right and multifamily housing is actually allowed. Coverage varies by state, so confirm completeness for any jurisdiction you rely on.
