Paper of the Week

★ Paper of the Week · published May 28, 2026
The Effect of Land Supply for New Homes on Residential Investment and House Prices
Justin Katz & Paul S. Willen · Federal Reserve Bank of Boston Working Paper No. 26-7
Why it's interesting: A complement to the dominant "blame regulation" story: using parcel-level data for New England (2007–2021), the authors show that physical land fragmentation independently constrains housing supply. Most buildable parcels are small and hemmed in by developed land; large parcels have grown scarcer, especially in populous, growing markets — and those markets see faster price growth and weaker development. Counterfactuals suggest recombining fragmented parcels into larger lots, holding total acreage fixed, would raise construction productivity, increase supply, and slow price growth. The policy implication is pointed: redeveloping large under-used government/commercial/industrial sites may beat scattered infill per acre.
Katz, Justin, and Paul S. Willen, The Effect of Land Supply for New Homes on Residential Investment and House Prices, Federal Reserve Bank of Boston Research Department Working Paper No. 26-7, May 2026.

Pioneer Institute · 2025
Why it's interesting: Parking minimums are among the most binding land-use constraints on achievable density; the study finds removing them materially expands feasible housing sites and cuts per-unit costs (structured parking runs roughly $34k–$59k per space above ground).
